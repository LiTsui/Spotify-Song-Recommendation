## **Project Overview**  
The NBA Series Predictor uses historical playoff game data and regular season team statistics to create matchup features and train a model capable of predicting which team will win the 2026 playoffs. The model will only use playoff data starting from 1996 since 1996 was the start of the "play by play era"; an era in which real-time statistics are measured game by game. 

The data has been partitioned into three datasets for model training. 
- The training dataset will use matchups from 1996 - 2019
- The validation dataset will use matchups from 2020 - 2022
- The testing dataset will use matchups from 2023 - 2025

The model compares two teams in a playoff series and predicts which team is more likely to advance. Instead of feeding in team statistics independently, the project creates difference-based features such as offensive rating difference, defensive rating difference, rebounding difference, and head-to-head win ratio.

Once first-round winners are predicted, the project rebuilds the bracket using those winners and repeats the process for the Conference Semifinals, Conference Finals, and NBA Finals. This creates a full simulation from the initial bracket to the championship round.

## **NBA Terminology**
The matchups for the NBA Playoffs are of a best of 7 format (the first round was a best of 5 until 2002). The games played to win a best of 7 are called a series. Therefore winning one game does not mean winning the series entirely. 

- **Seeds:** The rank of each team within their conference based on total wins during the regular season 
- **Western Conference:** Consists of 14 teams west of the Mississippi River and the Memphis Grizzlies 
- **Eastern Conference:** Consists of 15 teams east of the Mississippi River but not the Memphis Grizzlies
- **First Round:** The top 8 teams from each conference compete in a best of 7 (best of 5 until 2002) series for a spot in the Conference Semi-Finals
- **Conference Semi-Finals:** The second round of the NBA Playoffs. 4 teams from each conference compete in a best of 7 series for a spot in the Conference Finals 
- **Conference Finals:** The third round of the NBA Playoffs. 2 teams from each conference compete in a best of 7 series for a spot in the NBA Finals. 
- **NBA Finals:** The fourth and final round of the NBA Playoffs. 1 team from each conference compete in a best of 7 series for the NBA Championship and the Larry O’Brien Trophy

## Project structure

### Phase 1: Data collection

The project begins by pulling historical NBA team and matchup data, then storing both raw and cleaned versions locally. This stage focuses on collecting standings, playoff history, regular-season matchup data, and team-level statistical profiles.

### Phase 2: Data cleaning and feature engineering

After collection, the raw datasets are cleaned to handle invalid values and formatting inconsistencies. Matchup rows are then generated by comparing two teams directly across a shared feature set, producing difference-based model inputs.

### Phase 3: Model creation

A neural network is trained to classify whether the higher-seeded team in a matchup wins the series. The network takes engineered series features as input and outputs a prediction for the series winner.

### Phase 4: Validation tuning

Hyperparameters such as dropout rate and layer features are tuned using the validation split. This step is used to improve generalization before final evaluation.

### Phase 5: Testing

The tuned model is evaluated on playoff data from 2023-2025. This provides a cleaner estimate of how well the model performs on unseen series.

### Phase 6: 2026 playoff simulation

The final stage simulates the 2026 NBA playoffs round by round. After each round, the predicted winners are advanced into the next round of the tournament until a team wins the NBA Finals.

### **Limitations**
Injuries are an integral part of the NBA Playoffs and therefore a player’s individual impact is important to consider when predicting the outcome of a playoff series. However, due to the scale of this project, it is not plausible to assess every player’s impact on their team. It is also not plausible to predict which players would be injured. 

There are also instances where certain players may overperform or underperform during the playoffs compared to their regular season statistics.  A few examples would be Jimmy Butler while playing for the Miami Heat or James Harden.

### Extending the project

The program can be reused for future seasons once new data becomes available. To extend the project to another season, update the year bounds inside the main loops of `load_data.py`, `clean_data.py`, and `matchup_features.py`, then rerun those scripts in order.

Possible future improvements include:

- Accounting for possible injuries
- Better calibration of predicted probabilities
- Reduced feature redundancy
- Uncertainty estimates for each series from repeated simulations
- More interpretable models or feature analysis
- Compare results with XGBoost's decision tree architecture
- Consider K cross validation as an alternative to testing splits

### Notes

This project is focused on playoff **series** prediction, not single-game prediction. A team winning one game in a series does not imply that it will win the full series.

The model does not account for the Play-In Tournament. The project begins once the seeds for the playoffs are already known.
